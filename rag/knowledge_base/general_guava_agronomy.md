# General Guava Agronomy and Orchard Management

## Crop Identity
- **Common Name:** Guava
- **Scientific Name:** *Psidium guajava* L.
- **Family:** Myrtaceae
- **Origin:** Tropical America (Mexico, Central America, Caribbean)
- **Global Rank:** 4th most important tropical fruit crop (after Mango, Banana, Papaya)
- **India's Rank:** India is the world's largest producer of guava (approx. 44% of global production)
- **Major Producing States (India):** Uttar Pradesh, Madhya Pradesh, Bihar, Maharashtra, West Bengal, Andhra Pradesh, Gujarat

## Botanical Description
- **Growth habit:** Evergreen to semi-deciduous shrub or small tree (4–8 m height under cultivation)
- **Bark:** Smooth, thin, copper-colored, peeling in thin flakes — used as an ID feature
- **Leaves:** Simple, opposite, oblong-elliptic, 7–15 cm long; pinnate venation with 10–15 pairs of veins; aromatic when crushed
- **Flowers:** White, actinomorphic, 4-petalled; numerous stamens; axillary, solitary or in cymes
- **Fruit:** Berry, round to pear-shaped; 4–12 cm diameter; skin green to yellow; flesh white, pink, or red depending on variety; seeds small, hard, numerous
- **Flowering:** Twice per year in most regions (winter crop: June–July flowering; summer crop: February–March flowering)
- **Bearing:** Comes to bearing 3–4 years after planting; productive life 15–25 years

## Agroclimatic Requirements

### Temperature
- Optimal: 20–30°C for growth and fruiting
- Can tolerate: 5–45°C (frost-sensitive below 2°C; heat stress above 42°C)
- Chilling requirement: None — true tropical crop
- Cold damage: Temperatures below 2°C cause leaf and shoot damage; severe frost kills young trees

### Rainfall
- Optimal: 1,000–2,000 mm/year
- Can survive with: 500 mm/year with supplemental irrigation
- Waterlogging: Highly sensitive — even brief waterlogging causes root anaerobia and disease

### Altitude
- Grown commercially up to 1,500 m above sea level
- Best fruit quality at 200–800 m (balanced temperature and humidity)

### Soil Requirements
- **Preferred:** Well-drained sandy loam to loam soils
- **pH:** 6.0–7.5 (optimal); tolerates 4.5–8.2
- **Drainage:** Excellent drainage essential — roots are highly sensitive to anaerobic conditions
- **Soil depth:** Minimum 60 cm for commercial planting
- **Avoidance:** Heavy clay soils, saline soils (EC > 2.0 dS/m), very alkaline soils (pH > 8.0)

## Orchard Establishment

### Land Preparation
1. Deep ploughing (30–45 cm) to break hardpan and improve drainage
2. Levelling and layout — contour planting on slopes to prevent erosion
3. Pitting: Dig pits 60 cm × 60 cm × 60 cm per tree; fill with topsoil + 20 kg FYM + 1 kg SSP + 50 g Chlorpyrifos powder (for soil pest control)
4. Fumigate with Formaldehyde 2% solution if Fusarium wilt was present in previous crop
5. Allow pits to weather for 2–4 weeks before planting

### Planting
- **Spacing:** 6 × 6 m (278 trees/ha) standard; 8 × 8 m in high-fertility soils
- **High-density planting:** 3 × 3 m or 4 × 4 m under canopy management systems (400–1,100 trees/ha)
- **Season:** Onset of monsoon (June–July) for rainfed orchards; February–March for irrigated orchards
- **Planting material:** Vegetatively propagated (air layering, cutting, grafting) plants preferred over seedlings for variety uniformity

### Training and Pruning

#### Initial Training (Years 1–3)
- Allow a single, strong leader stem to develop
- Remove all lateral branches below 60–75 cm from ground level — creates a clear trunk
- After first harvest: Select 3–4 well-spaced primary scaffold branches; remove all others
- Annually prune to maintain an open "wine glass" canopy structure for light penetration

#### Annual Maintenance Pruning
- Prune immediately after harvest — removes fruited wood, stimulates new shoots for next cycle
- Remove: Dead wood, crossing branches, water shoots, inward-growing branches, diseased wood
- Apply Bordeaux paste (Copper sulfate 1 kg + Lime 1 kg + Water 10 L = thick paste) to all cut surfaces immediately after pruning

#### Pruning for Crop Regulation (Bahar Treatment)
- Guava flowers on new growth — by stressing the plant at a specific time, farmers can time the harvest
- **Mrig Bahar (Monsoon crop):** Stress in February–March (withhold irrigation for 40–60 days); crop matures December–January (premium prices)
- **Ambe Bahar (Summer crop):** Stress in November; crop matures June–July
- **Hasth Bahar (Winter crop):** Normal monsoon crop; most common

## Nutrition Management

### Macro-Nutrient Requirements (Per Tree Per Year — Bearing Tree)

| Nutrient | Rate | Source | Application Time |
|---------|------|--------|----------------|
| Nitrogen (N) | 260–500 g | Urea (46% N) | 3 split doses: Feb, June, Sept |
| Phosphorus (P₂O₅) | 250–350 g | SSP (16% P₂O₅) | Once, February |
| Potassium (K₂O) | 300–400 g | MOP or SOP | 2 splits: Feb, Aug |
| Organic matter | 30–50 kg | Well-decomposed FYM/compost | Pre-monsoon (May–June) |

### Micro-Nutrient Requirements

| Nutrient | Deficiency Symptom | Correction |
|---------|-------------------|-----------|
| Zinc (Zn) | Small leaves, stunted shoots, mottled yellowing | ZnSO₄ 0.3% foliar spray × 3 |
| Boron (B) | Flower drop, mummification, hollow fruits | Borax 0.3% foliar spray before flowering |
| Iron (Fe) | Interveinal chlorosis of young leaves | FeSO₄ 0.2% + citric acid 0.1% spray |
| Magnesium (Mg) | Interveinal chlorosis of older leaves | MgSO₄ 0.5% foliar spray × 3 |
| Calcium (Ca) | Fruit tip rot, poor cell wall integrity | Ca(NO₃)₂ 0.5% spray at fruit set |
| Manganese (Mn) | Interveinal chlorosis + grey flecks | MnSO₄ 0.2% foliar spray |

### Fertigation (Drip Irrigation Systems)
- Apply all water-soluble fertilizers through drip system
- Split into weekly doses for N and K; monthly for P and micronutrients
- Maintain EC of irrigation water below 1.0 dS/m

## Irrigation Management

### Water Requirements
- Daily ETc: 4–6 litres/tree/day (cool season) to 60–80 litres/tree/day (peak summer)
- Annual water requirement: 800–1,200 mm depending on variety and climate

### Irrigation Scheduling
| Season | Frequency | Method |
|--------|-----------|--------|
| Summer (March–June) | Every 3–4 days | Drip preferred |
| Monsoon | Rain-fed; supplement in dry spells | — |
| Post-monsoon (Oct–Dec) | Every 7–10 days | Drip or basin |
| Winter (Jan–Feb) | Every 14–21 days | Basin |

### Drip Irrigation Advantages
- Reduces leaf wetness → significant reduction in fungal disease incidence
- 40–60% water saving compared to flood irrigation
- Enables fertigation (fertilizer through drip)
- Prevents soil splash-mediated spread of soilborne pathogens

## Weed Management
- First 3 years (pre-bearing): Critical period — weeds compete intensely; weed 4–6 times per year manually or mechanically
- Bearing trees: Mow orchard floor regularly; maintain clean 1 m radius around tree trunk
- Herbicides: Glyphosate (non-selective) only as directed spray between rows — avoid contact with guava foliage or roots
- Mulching: 10 cm organic mulch around each tree base — suppresses weeds and retains moisture

## Harvest and Post-Harvest

### Maturity Indices
- **Visual:** Fruit turns from dark green to light green to yellow-green; slight yellowing at blossom end
- **Firmness:** Slight give when pressed with thumb; TSS (Brix) 9–14°
- **Days from flowering:** 90–120 days (variety dependent)

### Harvesting
- Hand-pick individually — do not allow fruits to drop (bruising triggers post-harvest rot)
- Harvest every 2–3 days at peak season — fruits mature rapidly
- Morning harvesting recommended — cooler temperatures reduce field heat

### Post-Harvest Handling
- Sort immediately for size, colour, and defects
- Dip in Carbendazim 0.05% solution for 2–3 minutes to control post-harvest rot
- Store at 8–10°C (ambient temperature shelf life: 3–5 days; cold storage: 2–3 weeks)
- Modified atmosphere packaging (MAP) with 3–5% CO₂ extends shelf life to 4 weeks

## Disease and Pest Management Calendar

| Month | Disease/Pest Risk | Recommended Action |
|-------|------------------|--------------------|
| Jan | Low (dry season) | Pruning + Bordeaux paste; soil testing |
| Feb | Thrips (new flush) | Spinosad spray; NPK fertilizer application |
| Mar–Apr | Mealybug; Aphids | Neem oil + insecticide; Biocontrol release |
| May (pre-monsoon) | Canker, Anthracnose | Copper oxychloride 0.3% prophylactic spray |
| June–Sept (monsoon) | All fungal diseases peak | Fortnightly fungicide alternation; drainage check |
| October | Rust (post-monsoon flush) | Propiconazole 1 ml/L; harvest sanitation |
| Nov–Dec | Fruit fly; Post-harvest diseases | Methyl eugenol traps; Carbendazim dip |

## Key Performance Indicators (Healthy Orchard Benchmarks)
- Fruit yield: 20–25 kg/tree/year (mature tree, 6 × 6 m spacing = 5,500–7,000 kg/acre)
- Disease incidence: <10% leaf area affected in well-managed orchards
- Fruit quality: TSS > 10 Brix; skin blemish < 5%
- Pest trap counts: <5 fruit flies per trap per week (below economic threshold)
