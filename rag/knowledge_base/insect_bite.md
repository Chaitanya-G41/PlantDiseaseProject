# Guava Insect Bite / Insect Damage

## Disease Identity
- **Common Name:** Insect Bite Damage, Insect Feeding Injury
- **Causal Agents (primary insects):**
  - *Bactrocera dorsalis* Hendel — Oriental Fruit Fly
  - *Frankliniella schultzei* Trybom / *Scirtothrips dorsalis* Hood — Thrips
  - *Planococcus citri* Risso — Citrus Mealybug; *Ferrisia virgata* Cockerell — Striped Mealybug
  - *Aphis gossypii* Glover — Cotton/Melon Aphid
  - *Idioscopus clypealis* Lethierry — Guava Leafhopper
  - *Leucoptera psidiella* (Leaf miner)
  - *Adoretus sinicus* Burmeister — Chinese Rose Beetle (defoliator)
- **Damage Type:** Mechanical feeding injury; also indirect damage via honeydew-induced sooty mold, pathogen entry through wounds, and virus/phytoplasma transmission by vectors
- **Host:** *Psidium guajava* L. (Guava)

## Overview
Insect feeding damage on guava leaves is one of the most common disorders observed in commercial orchards. Unlike fungal or bacterial diseases, insect damage is caused by physical feeding activity of pest insects — either chewing, piercing-sucking, or mining through leaf tissue. The resulting damage ranges from irregular holes and torn margins (chewing insects) to silvery streaks and stippling (thrips, mites) to leaf curling and honeydew deposits (aphids, mealybugs).

Critically, insect feeding wounds serve as primary entry points for secondary fungal and bacterial pathogens, particularly *Pestalotiopsis psidii* (canker), *Colletotrichum gloeosporioides* (anthracnose), and sooty mold fungi (*Capnodium* spp.). Additionally, sap-sucking insects such as leafhoppers and aphids are important vectors of guava phytoplasma (causing yellow leaf disease), making their management doubly important.

Economic significance of insect damage on guava is very high — the oriental fruit fly (*Bactrocera dorsalis*) alone can cause fruit losses of 30–80% in unmanaged orchards. Thrips and mealybugs are the principal leaf and shoot pests.

## Damage Symptoms by Pest Type

### Thrips (*Frankliniella schultzei*, *Scirtothrips dorsalis*)
- Silver-grey to bronze streaking on the adaxial (upper) leaf surface
- Leaf margin curling downward (rolling)
- Rasping-sucking injury causes characteristic "silvery sheen" on new growth
- Stunted, distorted new leaves; abort of flower buds in severe cases
- Thrips are tiny (1–2 mm), pale yellow to dark brown — look for them in folded leaf margins

### Mealybug (*Planococcus citri*, *Ferrisia virgata*)
- White, cottony, waxy masses on leaf undersides, shoot junctions, and bark crevices
- Yellowing and wilting of affected shoots
- Heavy honeydew secretion → black sooty mold growth on leaves
- Ants are a reliable indicator (they "farm" mealybugs for honeydew)
- Severe infestation causes shoot dieback

### Aphids (*Aphis gossypii*, *Toxoptera aurantii*)
- Dense colonies of soft-bodied insects on new growth and shoot tips
- Leaf curling and distortion of young growth
- Sticky honeydew deposits; secondary sooty mold development
- Yellowing of affected leaves; premature leaf drop in heavy infestations
- Aphids are primary vectors of guava mosaic virus

### Leafhoppers (*Idioscopus clypealis*)
- Stippled (dotted), pale green to yellow discoloration across the leaf blade
- Tiny white skin casts (exuviae) on the leaf underside
- Hopperburn — marginal necrosis in severe infestations
- Leafhoppers are vectors of phytoplasma causing yellow leaf disease

### Fruit Fly (*Bactrocera dorsalis*)
- Primarily a fruit pest; leaf damage is not typical
- Female flies puncture skin of ripening fruit to lay eggs → internal rotting
- Visible oviposition puncture marks on fruit surface
- On leaves: rarely observed but adults rest on leaves and may cause minor surface scratching

### Leaf Miners (*Leucoptera psidiella*)
- Silvery, serpentine (winding) mines visible through the leaf lamina
- Mines follow a characteristic zig-zag pattern
- Affected leaf areas turn brown and papery
- Heavily mined leaves drop prematurely

### Defoliators (Beetles, Caterpillars)
- Irregular holes in leaf blade
- Chewed margins — scalloped or ragged leaf edges
- Skeletal feeding (only veins remain) in severe caterpillar attack
- Frass (insect excrement) deposited on leaves below feeding sites

## Epidemiology and Favoring Conditions
- **Season:** Thrips and mealybugs peak during hot, dry weather (March–May); aphids peak in cool, dry weather; fruit flies peak during monsoon and fruiting season
- **Irrigation:** Excessive nitrogen fertilization promotes soft, succulent tissue preferred by sucking insects
- **Ant activity:** Ants actively protect mealybugs and aphids from natural enemies — monitor ant trails
- **Natural enemies:** Disruption of natural predator populations (by excessive broad-spectrum insecticide use) causes pest outbreaks

## Treatment Protocol

### Immediate Actions (On Detection)
1. Identify the specific pest — the treatment differs significantly between pest types
2. Assess infestation level: count pests per leaf or shoot to decide intervention threshold
3. Remove and bag heavily infested shoots and leaves
4. Control ant colonies (apply sticky barrier on tree trunks) — ants protect mealybugs and aphids from predators
5. Introduce or conserve natural enemies where possible before applying chemical controls

### Chemical Control by Pest

#### Thrips
- **Spinosad 45% SC** at 0.5 ml/L water — highly effective, low mammalian toxicity
- **Imidacloprid 70% WG** at 0.3 g/L water (systemic) — apply as foliar spray or soil drench
- **Fipronil 5% SC** at 1.5 ml/L water — use only for severe infestations; toxic to beneficial insects
- Pre-harvest interval: 14–21 days (check label)

#### Mealybug
- **Buprofezin 25% SC** at 1.5 ml/L water — insect growth regulator, safe for beneficial insects
- **Profenofos 50% EC** at 2 ml/L water — organophosphate, effective for heavy infestations
- **Dimethoate 30% EC** at 1.5 ml/L water — systemic; applied as soil drench reaches root mealybugs
- **Neem oil** 5 ml/L water with an emulsifier — disrupts mealybug waxy coat, safe option

#### Aphids
- **Flonicamid 50% WG** at 0.3 g/L water — highly selective against aphids; safe for pollinators
- **Acetamiprid 20% SP** at 0.15 g/L water — systemic neonicotinoid; quick knockdown
- **Neem oil** 5 ml/L + soap solution (2 ml/L) — effective for light infestations; repeat every 5–7 days
- **Insecticidal soap** 5 ml/L — contact action, safe organic option

#### Leafhoppers
- **Imidacloprid 17.8% SL** at 0.5 ml/L water — systemic, controls hoppers on new growth
- **Thiamethoxam 25% WG** at 0.2 g/L water — highly effective systemic neonicotinoid
- **Emamectin benzoate 5% SG** at 0.5 g/L — combined activity against hoppers and thrips

#### Fruit Fly
- **Protein hydrolysate bait + Malathion** (1% bait + 0.1% Malathion) — spot applications on bark and tree skirt
- **GF-120 NF Naturalyte** bait — commercially available fruit fly bait; apply as small spots (not whole-tree spray)
- **Methyl eugenol traps** — attract and kill male *B. dorsalis*; use 5 traps per acre; renew bait monthly

### Biological Control
- **Cryptolaemus montrouzieri** (mealybug destroyer beetle) — release 5–10 adults per infested tree for mealybug control
- **Chrysoperla carnea** (green lacewing) — release 1000–2000 eggs/acre against aphids, thrips, mealybug
- **Beauveria bassiana** (entomopathogenic fungus) at 5 g/L water — effective against thrips, mealybug, leafhoppers; apply in evening
- **Isaria fumosorosea** — effective against whiteflies and thrips
- **Parasitic wasps (Anagyrus lopezi)** — effective against mealybug species in released biocontrol programs

### Cultural Practices
- **Yellow sticky traps:** 2–4 per acre for thrips and aphid monitoring
- **Blue sticky traps:** Specifically attractive to thrips; use 5 per acre
- **Pheromone traps:** Methyl eugenol traps for fruit fly monitoring (5 per acre)
- Avoid excessive nitrogen fertilization — promotes succulent growth attractive to sucking insects
- Prune dense canopy to reduce microclimate humidity and pest shelter
- Remove weeds around trees — alternate hosts for thrips, aphids, and leafhoppers
- Practice orchard floor management — mow or clear vegetation during peak pest season
- Apply kaolin clay spray (5% suspension) — creates a physical barrier that deters thrips and fruit flies

## Integrated Pest Management (IPM) Calendar
| Month | Target Pest | Action |
|-------|-------------|--------|
| Feb–Mar | Thrips (on new flush) | Spinosad 0.5 ml/L OR Neem oil spray |
| April–May | Mealybug (peak season) | Buprofezin + Cryptolaemus release |
| June–Aug | Fruit fly (monsoon) | Methyl eugenol traps + protein bait |
| Sept–Oct | Aphids, Leafhoppers | Flonicamid + Chrysoperla release |
| Year-round | General monitoring | Sticky traps + weekly scouting |

## Thresholds for Chemical Intervention
- **Thrips:** >5 thrips per leaf or >20% new growth affected
- **Mealybug:** >5 mealybug colonies per tree or >10% shoot infestation
- **Aphids:** >20 aphids per shoot or >30% shoots affected
- **Leafhoppers:** >3 leafhoppers per sweep-net sample (10 sweeps)

## Economic Impact
- Fruit fly damage: 30–80% fruit loss in unmanaged orchards
- Mealybug: 20–40% reduction in fruit quality and yield
- Thrips: Up to 25% yield loss; significant downgrading of fruit quality
- IPM management cost: INR 2,000–4,000 per acre per season
