# Guava Leaf Scorch

## Disease Identity
- **Common Name:** Leaf Scorch, Leaf Burn, Marginal Leaf Scorch
- **Causal Agents:**
  - **Abiotic (Primary):** Water deficit / drought stress, heat stress, salt injury, chemical burn (herbicide/fertilizer), sunscald
  - **Nutritional (Abiotic):** Potassium (K) deficiency, Magnesium (Mg) deficiency, Calcium (Ca) deficiency, Iron (Fe) deficiency
  - **Biotic (Secondary / Mimicking):** *Erwinia psidii* (bacterial leaf scorch), Leafhopper-induced hopperburn, *Xanthomonas campestris* pv. *psidii*
  - **Root-related:** Root rot (*Phytophthora nicotianae*, *Pythium* spp.), root-knot nematode (*Meloidogyne* spp.) damage reducing water uptake
- **Disease Type:** Primarily abiotic (physiological) disorder; can have biotic components
- **Host:** *Psidium guajava* L. (Guava)
- **Important Note:** Leaf scorch in guava is NOT contagious in its primary abiotic form — it does not spread from plant to plant. However, if caused by bacterial agents or if scorched tissue is colonized by secondary pathogens, spread can occur.

## Overview
Leaf scorch in guava is a physiological disorder characterized by the browning and death of leaf margins and tips, while the interior of the leaf often remains green or turns yellow in later stages. The primary cause is an imbalance between water supply to the leaf and water demand (transpiration), resulting in tissue desiccation at the leaf periphery — where water delivery via the vascular system is most limited.

Leaf scorch is NOT a fungal or bacterial disease in its most common form. It is a stress response — a visible signal that the plant's water and/or nutrient supply chain is failing. This critical distinction guides treatment: antifungal or antibacterial sprays will have NO effect on abiotic scorch. The correct response is to identify and address the underlying cause — whether that is drought, soil compaction, root damage, nutrient deficiency, or excessive salt.

In commercial guava orchards, leaf scorch is most commonly caused by:
- Sudden transitions between wet and dry conditions
- Potassium or magnesium deficiency (most frequent nutritional cause)
- Root damage from nematodes, root rot fungi, or soil compaction
- Herbicide spray drift from nearby fields
- Over-application of fertilizers or pesticides causing salt/chemical burn

Understanding which type of scorch is present (drought, nutritional, chemical, root-related) is essential for effective treatment.

## Symptoms — Visual Diagnosis

### Classic Abiotic Leaf Scorch (Drought / Heat)
- **Margins first:** Brown, dry, papery necrosis begins at the leaf margin and tip — the area most distal from water supply
- **Progressive:** Scorching advances inward from the margin as stress intensifies
- **Color progression:** Pale yellow → tan → light brown → dark brown (the dry, papery texture is characteristic)
- **Pattern:** Affects older (basal) leaves first in potassium deficiency; younger leaves first in water stress
- **Texture:** Affected areas are dry, crisp, and fragile — break when handled
- **No lesion border:** Unlike fungal disease, there is NO dark border or halo around the scorch zone
- **Bilateral:** Both sides of the leaf are equally affected (unlike fungal spots which often start on one surface)

### Potassium Deficiency Scorch
- Scorching begins on the oldest (lowest) leaves — classic "bottom-up" progression
- Interveinal chlorosis (yellowing between veins) accompanies marginal scorch
- Veins remain green initially — striking visual contrast with yellow interveinal zones
- Margins brown from the tip inward
- Fruits on affected trees: small, misshapen, low Brix (sugar content)
- Soil test: K below 100 ppm is deficient for guava

### Magnesium Deficiency Scorch
- Interveinal chlorosis on middle-aged leaves (not oldest, not youngest)
- Characteristic "V-shaped" or "herringbone" pattern of green veins in a yellow/bronze leaf
- Marginal scorch in severe cases
- More common in sandy, acidic soils (Mg is leached easily)

### Iron Deficiency (Interveinal Chlorosis — Not Classic Scorch)
- Young (apical) leaves yellow first — "top-down" progression (opposite to K deficiency)
- Veins remain prominently green while leaf surface turns yellow to white
- Rarely causes marginal scorch; more commonly causes blanching/pale coloration
- Common in alkaline soils (pH > 7.5) where Fe is chemically unavailable

### Chemical / Salt Burn
- Sudden onset — symptoms appear within 24–72 hours of chemical application
- Affects leaves indiscriminately (all ages, all positions)
- Sharp, well-defined brown margins if from spray drift; diffuse if from soil salt accumulation
- Look for a "tide mark" — a distinct line between affected and healthy tissue
- Often affects one side of the tree (the side facing the spray source)

### Hopperburn (Biotic Scorch Mimic)
- Caused by leafhopper feeding on leaf margins and undersides
- Marginal browning very similar to abiotic scorch
- DIFFERENTIATOR: Look for leafhoppers, their white cast skins (exuviae) on the leaf underside, and tiny stippled feeding marks
- Hopperburn progresses rapidly when leafhopper populations are high

### Bacterial Leaf Scorch (*Erwinia psidii*)
- Causes water-soaked, dark lesions on margins that quickly necrose and dry
- Angular lesions bounded by leaf veins
- May be associated with bacterial streaming (visible under water microscopy)
- Spreads slowly from plant to plant via contaminated tools and rain splash

## Differential Diagnosis: Scorch vs. Fungal Diseases

| Feature | Leaf Scorch (Abiotic) | Canker (*Pestalotiopsis*) | Rust (*Puccinia*) |
|---------|----------------------|--------------------------|------------------|
| Location | Margins and tips | Interior leaf spots | Lower surface pustules |
| Color | Brown-tan, papery | Dark brown-black spots | Bright orange powder |
| Border | No defined border | Dark border / yellow halo | Chlorotic yellow halo |
| Texture | Dry, crisp, fragile | Lesion may be raised | Powdery pustules |
| Progression | Margin inward | Spot expansion | Pustule multiplication |
| Fungal bodies | NONE | Pycnidia visible | Uredinia (pustules) |
| Contagious? | NO (abiotic) | YES | YES |

## Epidemiology and Favoring Conditions

### Abiotic Scorch
- **Summer months** (April–June): High evapotranspiration demand; if irrigation is insufficient, scorch develops rapidly
- **Post-monsoon dry spell** (October–November): Sudden moisture deficit after a long wet season
- **Sandy soils:** Poor water and potassium retention; high leaching
- **Alkaline soils (pH > 7.5):** Fe, Mn, Zn, B are chemically locked — deficiency symptoms manifest as scorch
- **High wind:** Increases transpiration demand, intensifying drought-induced scorch

### Root-Associated Scorch
- Root-knot nematodes (*Meloidogyne incognita*) damage fine roots — reduces water uptake capacity → canopy scorch even when soil is moist
- *Phytophthora* root rot — identical presentation; look for dark root discoloration

## Treatment Protocol

### Step 1: Accurate Diagnosis
- Check soil moisture and irrigation history
- Review recent spray applications (herbicide drift?)
- Pull up a few fine roots — are they healthy white, or dark and rotted?
- Check soil pH
- Look for leafhopper adults/exuviae on leaf underside

### Step 2: Irrigation Correction
- If drought-induced: Begin deep, slow irrigation immediately
  - Apply 60–80 litres per tree; allow water to penetrate 30–40 cm deep
  - Switch to drip irrigation if possible — delivers water directly to root zone
  - Apply thick mulch layer (10 cm paddy straw or dry leaves) around the tree base to reduce evaporation
- Avoid flood irrigation — creates anaerobic conditions harmful to roots
- Irrigation frequency: Every 3–4 days in summer; every 6–8 days in moderate weather

### Step 3: Nutritional Correction

#### Potassium Deficiency
- **Immediate foliar spray:** Potassium nitrate (KNO₃) 0.5% (5 g/L water) — 2–3 applications at 10-day intervals
- **Soil application:** Muriate of potash (MOP, KCl) or Potassium sulfate (SOP, K₂SO₄) — 300–400 g per tree; apply in split doses with irrigation
- Potassium sulfate is preferred in saline soils (avoids adding chloride)

#### Magnesium Deficiency
- **Foliar spray:** Magnesium sulfate (MgSO₄, Epsom salt) 0.5% (5 g/L water) — 3 sprays at 10-day intervals
- **Soil application:** Dolomitic limestone (MgCO₃·CaCO₃) at 1–2 kg per tree if soil pH correction is also needed

#### Iron Deficiency (Alkaline Soils)
- **Foliar spray:** Ferrous sulfate 0.2% (2 g/L water) with a few drops of citric acid (0.1%) to keep Fe in solution
- **Chelated iron (Fe-EDTA or Fe-EDDHA):** 1–2 g/L as foliar spray — more effective in alkaline conditions; more expensive but superior uptake
- **Soil acidification:** Apply elemental sulfur (200 g/tree) to reduce soil pH and improve Fe availability

#### General Micronutrient Correction
- Foliar spray of complete micronutrient mixture: Zinc sulfate 0.3% + Boron (borax) 0.2% + Magnesium sulfate 0.5% + Ferrous sulfate 0.2% — apply as tank mix (check compatibility)

### Step 4: Root Health Restoration
- If root-knot nematode detected (galls on roots): Apply Carbofuran 3G (30 g per tree) or Nematode-antagonistic *Paecilomyces lilacinus* (5 g/L as soil drench)
- If root rot (*Phytophthora*) detected: Soil drench with Metalaxyl 8% + Mancozeb 64% WP at 2.5 g/L; improve drainage urgently
- Drench *Trichoderma harzianum* (5 g/L) around root zone — promotes root recovery and disease suppression

### Step 5: Treat Hopperburn (if biotic)
- Spray Thiamethoxam 25% WG at 0.2 g/L OR Imidacloprid 17.8% SL at 0.5 ml/L
- Cover leaf underside thoroughly — leafhoppers congregate there

## Preventive Measures
- **Soil testing every 2 years** — correct K, Mg, pH proactively
- **Mulching** (10 cm organic mulch) — reduces soil temperature, conserves moisture, prevents drought scorch
- **Annual potassium application** — even without visible symptoms; guava is a high-K-demand crop
- **Regular irrigation scheduling** — do not allow soil to dry below 50% field capacity
- **Calibrate spray equipment** — avoid pesticide/herbicide overdose; check nozzle drift during application
- **Nematode monitoring** — inspect root health annually; apply biocontrol proactively in nematode-prone soils

## Economic Impact
- Drought/nutritional scorch: 10–30% yield reduction due to reduced photosynthetic area and fruit quality degradation
- Root nematode-driven scorch: Progressive — yield decline of 5–10% per year if untreated; tree death in 5–8 years
- Correct diagnosis avoids unnecessary fungicide costs (INR 3,000–6,000 per acre) spent on non-fungal scorch
