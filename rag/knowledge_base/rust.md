# Guava Rust

## Disease Identity
- **Common Name:** Guava Rust, Eucalyptus-Guava Rust, American Guava Rust
- **Causal Organism:** *Puccinia psidii* G. Winter (syn. *Austropuccinia psidii*)
- **Disease Type:** Fungal (obligate biotrophic rust pathogen)
- **Host:** *Psidium guajava* L. (Guava) — also infects Myrtaceae family members including Eucalyptus, Allspice, Clove
- **Classification:** Foliar rust (urediniospore-forming)
- **Quarantine Status:** Listed as a quarantine pest in several countries (USA-USDA, EPPO); subject to phytosanitary restrictions on guava plant imports

## Overview
Guava rust (*Puccinia psidii*) is one of the most threatening fungal pathogens of guava and the broader Myrtaceae family globally. The disease, commonly known as "American Rust" or "Eucalyptus Rust," is capable of causing near-complete defoliation within a single season under favorable conditions. It is endemic to the Americas (Brazil, Colombia, South America) and has spread to Hawaii, Japan, South Africa, and Australia, causing significant ecological and agricultural concern.

The pathogen is an obligate biotroph, meaning it can only grow and sporulate on living plant tissue. It produces massive quantities of yellow-orange powdery spores (urediniospores) that are dispersed over long distances by wind, making its spread rapid and difficult to contain. Young, actively growing shoots and leaves are the most susceptible; mature, hardened leaves show significantly greater resistance.

In India, guava rust outbreaks have been reported in states with high humidity and guava cultivation (Maharashtra, Karnataka, Gujarat, Andhra Pradesh), and the disease is considered an emerging threat to commercial guava production.

## Symptoms

### Early Stage (Days 1–5 post-infection)
- Small, pale yellow spots (1–3 mm) appearing on the adaxial (upper) leaf surface
- Corresponding pale greenish-yellow uredia visible on the abaxial (lower) leaf surface
- Water-soaked appearance around the infection site

### Active Sporulation Stage (Days 5–14)
- Yellow-orange to bright orange powdery pustules (uredinia) on the lower leaf surface — highly characteristic
- Upper leaf surface shows corresponding yellow to orange spots
- Pustules rupture, releasing masses of orange urediniospores — visible as orange powder on hands when touched
- Surrounding leaf tissue turns yellow-green (chlorotic)

### Advanced / Severe Stage
- Pustules coalesce, forming large yellow-orange patches covering the entire leaf
- Massive defoliation — leaf fall within 2–3 weeks of heavy infection
- Dieback of young shoots
- Reduced flower and fruit set
- Stunted plant growth
- In nurseries: complete death of seedlings within weeks

### Fruit Symptoms (Rare, Severe Cases)
- Small orange pustules on immature fruit surface
- Premature fruit drop

## Causal Agent Details
- *Puccinia psidii* produces three spore types: urediniospores (asexual, yellow-orange), teliospores (sexual, dark brown), and basidiospores
- Urediniospores: 18–28 × 15–22 μm, ellipsoidal, echinulate (spiny) surface, 2–4 germ pores
- The pathogen has multiple races/strains with varying host specificity and virulence
- Optimal temperature for infection: **18–25°C** (cool to warm conditions); infection is poor above 30°C
- Optimal relative humidity: **>90%** — free water or prolonged leaf wetness (6+ hours) required for spore germination
- Infection is **fastest on young, expanding leaves** (3–10 days old); mature leaves are largely resistant

## Epidemiology and Favoring Conditions
- **Climate:** Cool, humid, cloudy weather is ideal; tropical highland regions and coastal humid zones are high-risk
- **Temperature:** 18–25°C optimal; the disease is suppressed above 30°C
- **Humidity:** High relative humidity (>85%) and free moisture on leaves (dew, rain) are critical
- **Season:** Post-monsoon (October–December) and winter flush periods are peak risk in India
- **Host stage:** New vegetative flushes are the most vulnerable — young leaves are highly susceptible
- **Wind dispersal:** Urediniospores can travel hundreds of kilometres on wind currents — long-distance spread
- **Altitude:** Disease is more severe at higher altitudes (cooler temperatures, higher humidity)

## Treatment Protocol

### Immediate Actions (On Detection)
1. **Isolate the area** — do not transport infected plant material, soil, or equipment from infected to healthy areas
2. Remove and carefully bag infected leaves and shoots — do NOT shake plants (urediniospores are easily dislodged and can spread)
3. Burn all collected infected material; do not compost
4. Report severe outbreaks to local agricultural department (disease has quarantine significance)
5. Disinfect footwear, tools, and clothing before moving between plots

### Chemical Control
- **First-line:** Propiconazole (triazole) 25% EC at 1 ml/L water — systemic, highly effective against rusts; apply at the first sign of symptoms
- **Alternative:** Tebuconazole 25.9% EC at 1 ml/L water — excellent systemic rust control
- **Broad-spectrum protectant:** Mancozeb 75% WP at 0.25% (2.5 g/L) — use as preventive spray before disease onset
- **Copper-based:** Copper oxychloride 50% WP at 0.3% (3 g/L) — protectant, less effective on established rust but good for prevention
- **Application schedule:** Apply at 10–14 day intervals; begin at first sign of disease or at flush stage (when new leaves are emerging)
- **Spray coverage:** Ensure thorough coverage of the lower leaf surface where pustules are formed
- **Resistance management:** Rotate triazoles (Propiconazole, Tebuconazole) with Mancozeb or sulfur-based fungicides

### Biological Control
- Sulfur-based fungicides (Wettable Sulfur 80% WP at 2.5 g/L) — effective at early stages; avoid in hot weather (>32°C) to prevent phytotoxicity
- *Trichoderma* soil drenches — not directly effective against rust but improve overall plant health and resistance
- Induced resistance: Salicylic acid spray (0.1–0.5 mM) has been reported to activate plant systemic acquired resistance (SAR) against *P. psidii* in research settings

### Cultural Practices
- Avoid dense plantings — maintain adequate tree spacing (5 × 5 m to 6 × 6 m) for air circulation
- Time pruning to avoid synchronized flushing of new growth during high-disease-risk periods
- Avoid overhead irrigation — minimizes leaf wetness duration
- Monitor new growth flushes weekly — intervene at first sign of orange pustules
- Windbreaks can reduce but not eliminate aerially dispersed spores

## Preventive Measures
- **Pre-flush spray:** Apply Mancozeb 0.25% or Copper oxychloride 0.3% before new growth flushes emerge during risk periods (post-monsoon)
- **Resistant varieties:** Research is ongoing; no fully resistant commercial guava variety is available; Israel varieties (e.g., Thai Guava) show moderate tolerance
- **Nursery hygiene:** Strict quarantine at nursery level — do not import planting material from rust-endemic regions without certification
- **Early detection:** Train farm workers to recognize the distinctive orange powder symptom — early intervention (within 5 days of infection) is far more effective than late treatment
- **Monitoring:** Check new growth flushes daily during cool, humid weather

## Risk Assessment for India
- **High-risk states:** Maharashtra (Western Ghats), Karnataka (coastal), Kerala, Andhra Pradesh (coastal), Uttarakhand (hilly guava tracts)
- **Climate change impact:** Increasing rainfall variability and cooler post-monsoon periods may increase disease incidence
- **Exotic races:** Introduction of more virulent races from the Americas or Australia poses an ongoing risk

## Economic Impact
- Yield loss: 40–80% in severe epidemic years
- Can cause complete crop failure in nurseries
- High fungicide cost in endemic areas: INR 6,000–12,000 per acre per season during outbreaks
- Long-term loss: Repeated severe defoliation weakens trees and reduces productive lifespan
