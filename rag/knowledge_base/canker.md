# Guava Canker

## Disease Identity
- **Common Name:** Guava Canker, Guava Fruit Canker
- **Causal Organism:** *Pestalotiopsis psidii* (de Not.) Steyaert (anamorph); also *Glomerella psidii* and *Colletotrichum psidii* in some regional reports
- **Disease Type:** Fungal (necrotrophic pathogen)
- **Host:** *Psidium guajava* L. (Guava)
- **Classification:** Leaf blight, stem canker, fruit rot

## Overview
Guava canker is one of the most economically important fungal diseases of guava orchards in tropical and subtropical regions. The disease is caused primarily by *Pestalotiopsis psidii*, a necrotrophic fungal pathogen that infects leaves, stems, branches, and fruits. It is prevalent under conditions of high humidity, dense canopy, and poorly managed orchards. The pathogen produces characteristic dark, sunken necrotic lesions that expand rapidly under favorable conditions. Guava canker can cause yield losses of 20–40% if left unmanaged, and is particularly destructive during the monsoon season when prolonged leaf wetness facilitates spore germination and infection.

The fungus survives as mycelium and conidia in infected plant debris, mummified fruits, and bark of infected branches. Spores are disseminated via wind, rain splash, and contaminated farm implements. Entry into plant tissue occurs primarily through wounds, natural openings (lenticels, stomata), and insect feeding damage.

## Symptoms

### Leaf Symptoms
- Small, circular to irregular, dark brown to black necrotic spots (2–10 mm diameter) on both leaf surfaces
- Lesions surrounded by a chlorotic (yellow) halo in early stages of infection
- As lesions mature, the center becomes pale tan to ash-grey with a dark brown border
- Multiple lesions coalesce to form large irregular blighted areas
- Premature leaf drop (defoliation) in severe cases
- Acervuli (dark fruiting bodies of the fungus) visible as tiny black dots in aged lesions under magnification

### Stem and Branch Symptoms
- Elongated, sunken, dark brown to black cankers on young shoots and branches
- Girdling of branches in severe infection, causing wilting and dieback
- Bark cracking with exudation of gum (gummosis) in some genotypes
- Presence of pycnidia or sporodochia (fungal fruiting structures) on lesion surface

### Fruit Symptoms
- Small, water-soaked lesions that turn dark brown to black
- Lesions are sunken with raised margins
- Fruit rots and mummifies in severe cases
- Secondary invasion by saprophytic fungi causes complete fruit loss

## Causal Agent Details
- *Pestalotiopsis psidii* produces three-celled conidia with two apical setulae (hair-like appendages)
- Acervuli appear as small, black, cushion-like structures on infected tissue
- Optimal growth temperature: 25–28°C
- High relative humidity (>85%) and prolonged leaf wetness (>6 hours) favour infection
- The pathogen has a wide host range but is most virulent on guava

## Epidemiology and Favoring Conditions
- **Temperature:** 25–30°C (optimal for spore germination and infection)
- **Humidity:** Relative humidity above 80% is a critical trigger
- **Rainfall:** High rainfall and frequent overcast conditions accelerate spread
- **Season:** Monsoon and post-monsoon periods (June–October in India) are peak disease periods
- **Orchard density:** Dense plantings with poor air circulation intensify the disease
- **Wounding:** Pruning wounds, insect feeding wounds, hailstorm damage serve as infection courts
- **Soil conditions:** Waterlogged soils weaken plant immunity and increase susceptibility

## Treatment Protocol

### Immediate Actions (On Detection)
1. Inspect the entire orchard — mark affected trees for targeted treatment
2. Prune and remove all infected branches, at least 10–15 cm below the visible lesion margin
3. Sterilize pruning tools with 70% ethanol or 10% bleach solution between cuts to prevent mechanical spread
4. Collect all pruned material, fallen infected leaves, and mummified fruits in sealed bags
5. Do NOT compost infected material — burn or bury away from the orchard
6. Apply wound dressing (Bordeaux paste or commercial wound sealant) to pruning cuts immediately

### Chemical Control
- **First-line fungicide:** Copper oxychloride (COC) at 0.3% (3 g/L water) — spray to run-off on leaves, stems, and fruits
- **Alternative:** Mancozeb 75% WP at 0.25% (2.5 g/L water) — broad-spectrum protectant
- **Systemic option:** Carbendazim 50% WP at 0.1% (1 g/L water) — for established infections with systemic spread
- **Application frequency:** Every 10–15 days during the active disease period (monsoon season)
- **Pre-harvest interval:** 7–14 days (check product label)
- **Rotation:** Alternate between contact (Copper/Mancozeb) and systemic (Carbendazim) fungicides every 2–3 sprays to prevent resistance development
- **Spray timing:** Early morning or evening; avoid spraying in strong sunlight or when rain is imminent within 4 hours

### Biological Control
- *Trichoderma viride* or *T. harzianum* at 5 g/L water as soil drench around the root zone — promotes soil suppressiveness and induced systemic resistance
- *Bacillus subtilis*-based biocontrol agents (e.g., Serenade) at label-recommended rates — can be used as foliar spray
- *Pseudomonas fluorescens* at 10 g/L as foliar spray — antifungal secondary metabolite production
- Biocontrol agents should be applied in the evening and not mixed with chemical fungicides

### Cultural Practices
- Prune orchards after harvest to improve canopy aeration; target a tree height that allows 50–60% light penetration through the canopy
- Avoid overhead irrigation — convert to drip or furrow irrigation to minimize foliar wetness
- Remove and destroy fallen leaves from the orchard floor weekly during the monsoon
- Avoid planting guava in low-lying, poorly drained areas
- Maintain orchard hygiene: remove all crop debris within 2 weeks of harvest
- Avoid injury to plants during intercultural operations

## Preventive Measures
- **Pre-monsoon spray** (May–June): Apply Bordeaux mixture (1%) or Copper oxychloride (0.3%) 2–3 weeks before the monsoon to create a protective chemical barrier
- **Resistant varieties:** L-49 (Lucknow-49, Sardar variety) shows moderate tolerance; select locally recommended resistant cultivars
- **Nursery hygiene:** Use disease-free certified planting material; inspect nursery plants before transplanting
- **Wind barriers:** Plant windbreaks (e.g., Casuarina, Eucalyptus) on the windward side to reduce wind-driven spore dispersal
- **Micronutrient management:** Maintain adequate zinc and boron levels through foliar sprays — micronutrient-deficient plants are more susceptible

## Integrated Disease Management (IDM) Schedule
| Month | Practice |
|-------|----------|
| March–April | Post-harvest pruning + Bordeaux paste on wounds |
| May (pre-monsoon) | Bordeaux mixture 1% spray (prophylactic) |
| June–September | Mancozeb 0.25% + Copper oxychloride 0.3% alternating every 14 days |
| October | Carbendazim 0.1% systemic spray if disease persists |
| November–February | Orchard sanitation, soil drench with Trichoderma |

## Economic Impact
- Yield loss: 20–40% in susceptible varieties under unmanaged conditions
- Post-harvest losses due to fruit canker: up to 30%
- Management cost: approximately INR 3,000–5,000 per acre per season
