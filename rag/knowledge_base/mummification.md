# Guava Mummification

## Disease Identity
- **Common Name:** Guava Mummification, Fruit Mummification, Twig and Shoot Mummification
- **Causal Organisms (primary):**
  - *Pestalotiopsis psidii* (de Not.) Steyaert — fungal pathogen (also causes canker)
  - *Colletotrichum gloeosporioides* (Penz.) Penz. & Sacc. — anthracnose pathogen
  - *Phomopsis psidii* — associated with fruit and twig mummification
  - *Botryodiplodia theobromae* Pat. — associated with dieback and mummification
  - Secondary: *Aspergillus* spp., *Penicillium* spp. (saprophytic colonizers of mummified tissue)
- **Abiotic Contribution:** Severe water stress, heat stress, or boron deficiency can trigger physiological mummification in young fruits and shoots
- **Disease Type:** Fungal (necrotrophic); also physiological (abiotic stress-induced)
- **Host:** *Psidium guajava* L. (Guava)
- **Affected Parts:** Young fruits, immature shoots, flower buds, developing seeds

## Overview
Mummification in guava refers to the desiccation and shrivelling of plant tissue — most characteristically young fruits, flower buds, and shoot tips — resulting in a dried, hardened, "mummified" appearance. The affected organs fail to develop, turn dark brown to black, and often remain attached to the plant in a shrivelled state rather than falling off, which is the origin of the term "mummification."

Two distinct processes can cause mummification in guava:

**1. Biotic (Fungal) Mummification:** Fungal pathogens — particularly *Pestalotiopsis psidii*, *Colletotrichum gloeosporioides*, and *Phomopsis psidii* — infect young fruits or shoot tips, causing rapid tissue necrosis and desiccation. The pathogen mycelium colonizes the internal tissue, causing progressive cell death, water loss, and the characteristic mummified appearance. Mummified fruits serve as primary inoculum reservoirs; the pathogens sporulate on the mummified surface, releasing spores that infect new growth.

**2. Abiotic (Physiological) Mummification:** Severe drought stress, extreme heat, or boron deficiency can cause young fruit drop followed by mummification of abscised or retained organs. In this case, no pathogen is involved — the damage is purely physiological. Differentiating between biotic and abiotic mummification is critical for correct management.

## Symptoms

### Fruit Mummification
- Young (marble-sized) fruits fail to develop and begin to shrivel within 2–4 weeks of fruit set
- Affected fruits turn from pale green to dark brown to black progressively
- Fruits become hard, leathery, completely desiccated — shriveled to 20–30% of normal size
- Mummified fruits either hang on the tree for weeks or drop prematurely
- Surface of mummified fruits may show small black fruiting bodies (pycnidia/acervuli) of the causal fungus
- In *Colletotrichum*-induced mummification: salmon-pink to orange spore masses (sporodochia) visible on the surface under humid conditions
- Internal tissue: completely brown, desiccated, with no viable seed development

### Shoot Tip and Twig Mummification
- Growing shoot tips suddenly wilt, darken, and desiccate — terminal shoot death (dieback)
- Affected twigs turn brown to black; bark shrivels
- Leaves on affected shoots dry and remain attached in a "blighted" appearance
- Dark, sunken lesions at the base of affected shoots — extending proximally toward main branch
- Fungal pycnidia or conidia masses visible on bark of dead twigs

### Flower Bud Mummification
- Flower buds fail to open, turn brown and shrivel before or at anthesis (flower opening)
- Significant impact on fruit set — reduces yield directly
- Mummified buds may show fungal sporulation at the tip

### Leaf Symptoms (Associated)
- Leaves adjacent to mummified shoots show marginal scorching and early senescence
- Spot lesions consistent with *Pestalotiopsis* or *Colletotrichum* may be co-present

## Differentiating Biotic vs. Abiotic Mummification

| Feature | Biotic (Fungal) | Abiotic (Stress) |
|---------|----------------|-----------------|
| Visible fungal bodies | Yes (pycnidia, acervuli) | No |
| Pattern | Scattered, random distribution | Affects sun-exposed side or entire canopy uniformly |
| Season | Monsoon, post-monsoon | Summer (heat/drought), irregular |
| Sporulation | Visible orange/black spore masses | Absent |
| Associated symptoms | Other fungal lesions on leaves/stems | Wilting, soil cracking, leaf scorch |
| Lab diagnosis | Fungal culture from mummified tissue | Boron/nutrient analysis of tissue/soil |

## Causal Agent Details

### *Pestalotiopsis psidii*
- Produces oblong, 5-celled conidia with 2–4 apical setulae (see Canker document for full details)
- Colonizes mummified tissue extensively; can survive for months in mummified fruits
- Primary inoculum for new fruit infections

### *Colletotrichum gloeosporioides*
- Causes anthracnose and quiescent (latent) fruit infections during development
- Infection may occur at flower or very young fruit stage; symptoms appear as fruit matures
- Orange sporodochia visible on surface under humid conditions
- Optimal infection temperature: 28–32°C

### *Phomopsis psidii*
- Causes fruit rot and shoot dieback
- Produces flask-shaped pycnidia on infected plant material
- Less studied but increasingly associated with mummification in South Asian orchards

### *Botryodiplodia theobromae*
- Wide host range; primarily a stress-pathogen (attacks weakened plants)
- Causes stem-end rot, dieback, and mummification under high-temperature, post-flooding conditions
- Produces dark, thick-walled pycnidia on dead wood

## Epidemiology and Favoring Conditions
- **Rainfall:** High rainfall during flowering and early fruit set; monsoon conditions promote spore dispersal
- **Temperature:** 25–32°C optimal for biotic mummification pathogens
- **Humidity:** >80% RH required for spore germination and infection
- **Plant stress:** Drought, waterlogging, or nutritional stress predisposes plants to abiotic mummification and increases susceptibility to biotic mummification
- **Orchard hygiene:** Presence of mummified fruits and infected debris from previous seasons is the primary inoculum source
- **Pruning wounds:** Entry points for *Phomopsis* and *Botryodiplodia*

## Treatment Protocol

### Immediate Actions (On Detection)
1. Remove ALL mummified fruits and infected shoot tips from the tree — do not leave them hanging (they are inoculum reservoirs)
2. Collect mummified material in sealed plastic bags; destroy by burning or deep burial (>30 cm)
3. Sterilize all pruning tools before and after use (70% alcohol or 10% bleach)
4. Inspect adjacent trees — mummification often spreads from a focal point
5. Send a sample to a plant pathology lab if aetiology is unclear (biotic vs. abiotic)

### Chemical Control (Biotic Mummification)
- **Propiconazole 25% EC** at 1 ml/L water — effective systemic fungicide for all three causal fungi; apply at pea-sized fruit stage
- **Carbendazim 50% WP** at 0.1% (1 g/L water) — systemic; penetrates infected tissue
- **Tebuconazole 25.9% EC** at 1 ml/L water — triazole systemic; excellent spectrum
- **Copper oxychloride 50% WP** at 0.3% (3 g/L water) — contact protectant; apply at flowering stage to protect young fruits
- **Application schedule:**
  - Spray 1: At 50% flowering (protectant — Copper oxychloride or Mancozeb)
  - Spray 2: At fruit set (pea stage) — Carbendazim or Propiconazole
  - Spray 3: 15 days later if disease persists — alternate with Tebuconazole
- **Wound treatment:** Apply Bordeaux paste or Copper oxychloride paste to pruning cuts on infected shoots

### Treatment for Abiotic Mummification
- **Boron deficiency:** Apply borax (0.3% solution = 3 g/L water) as foliar spray 2–3 times before and during flowering
- **Drought stress:** Improve irrigation frequency; apply mulch; use anti-transpirants (kaolin 5% spray) during peak summer
- **Calcium deficiency:** Apply calcium nitrate 0.5% foliar spray at fruit set — reduces fruit drop and mummification
- **Heat stress:** Provide temporary shade nets (50% shade) over nursery plants; mature trees are less amenable to shading

### Biological Control
- *Trichoderma harzianum* soil drench (5 g/L) — suppresses soilborne *Colletotrichum* and *Botryodiplodia* spore populations
- *Bacillus subtilis* foliar spray — produces iturin and surfactin antibiotics effective against *Colletotrichum* and *Pestalotiopsis*
- Maintain orchard biodiversity — conserve beneficial predatory insects and fungi

### Cultural Practices
- **Pre-flowering sanitation:** Remove all mummified fruits and dead twigs from the previous season BEFORE new flowering begins — this is the single most important management practice
- Improve soil drainage — waterlogged soil dramatically increases *Botryodiplodia* and *Phomopsis* activity
- Avoid wounding plant tissue during canopy management operations near flowering
- Ensure balanced NPK + boron + calcium nutrition — deficiencies directly increase susceptibility
- Prune after harvest to remove all dead and infected wood; apply Bordeaux paste to all cut surfaces

## Preventive Measures
- **Pre-monsoon spray** (May–June): Copper oxychloride 0.3% — forms a protective barrier before rainfall-induced spore dispersal
- **At-flowering preventive spray:** Mancozeb 0.25% or Carbendazim 0.05% — protects flower buds and young fruits at their most susceptible stage
- **Boron supplementation:** Annual borax spray (0.3%) at pre-flowering stage — prevents physiological mummification due to boron deficiency
- **Orchard floor hygiene:** Collect and destroy all fallen mummified fruits within 1 week — these are primary inoculum sources for the next season
- **Certified planting material:** Use nursery plants free from *Phomopsis* and *Colletotrichum* infection

## Economic Impact
- Young fruit mummification: 15–50% loss in fruit set depending on disease pressure
- Post-harvest fruit mummification: Significant downgrading and rejection during marketing
- Shoot dieback: Reduces productive canopy area, impacting long-term yield
- Management cost: INR 3,000–6,000 per acre per season
